Makassar

About Makassar

Makassar is the capital city of South Sulawesi, Indonesia, and is the largest city in eastern Indonesia. It has long been a vital hub for trade and cultural exchange, given its strategic location on historic maritime routes. The city blends diverse influences from across Indonesia, Asia, and Europe, making it a melting pot of cultures. Makassar is known for its stunning waterfronts, such as the Losari Beach, and its rich culinary traditions, including the famous Coto Makassar and Pisang Ijo. Historically, it was the seat of the powerful Gowa Sultanate and home to the iconic Fort Rotterdam, a well-preserved Dutch colonial fortress. The city has a vibrant cultural heritage, particularly evident in its traditional dance, music, and festivals like the Pa'jukukang festival, celebrating the region's maritime legacy. Makassar is also a gateway to popular tourist destinations, such as the Tana Toraja highlands and the pristine beaches of Bulukumba. Its blend of modernity and history makes it a fascinating place to explore.

Historical Significance

Fort Rotterdam, also known as Benteng Ujung Pandang, is a well-preserved 17th-century fortress located in Makassar, South Sulawesi, Indonesia. Built by the Gowa Sultanate, the fort was initially called Benteng Panyyua and served as the seat of power for the Kingdom of Gowa, one of the most influential maritime kingdoms in eastern Indonesia.

History:

  • Original Construction: The fort was built in the mid-16th century by Sultan Alauddin, the ruler of Gowa, from local stone and materials. It was originally constructed in a traditional Gowa architectural style and served as a military stronghold to protect the kingdom from external forces.
  • Dutch Takeover: In 1667, after the defeat of the Gowa Sultanate in the Treaty of Bungaya, the fort was handed over to the Dutch East India Company (VOC). The Dutch renovated it, transforming it into a European-style fort and renaming it Fort Rotterdam. The fort then became a central hub for Dutch colonial administration in the region.
  • Prison of Prince Diponegoro: The fort is historically significant as it was the place where Prince Diponegoro, a key figure in the Java War (1825-1830), was imprisoned after his capture by the Dutch.
